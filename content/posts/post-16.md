#### Levels 2 & 3

The one-event hedge in part two was the standard way to convert a single bonus bet into real cash. A more elaborate method becomes available when the bettor can divide bonus money across many wagers. The idea is to select \(N\) binary events, construct a bonus-funded parlay for every possible realization of those events, and distribute the bonus stake across those parlays so that exactly one ticket wins and the resulting cash conversion is guaranteed. This post formalizes that method and characterizes when it's optimal.

## Setup

Suppose the bettor has total bonus money \(B>0\) that may be split across multiple bonus wagers. Let there be \(N\) binary events, indexed by \(j=1,\dots,N\). For each event \(j\), the two outcomes are labeled \(0\) and \(1\), with decimal odds \(d_{j,0}>1\) and \(d_{j,1}>1\). The terminal outcome space is \(\Omega=\{0,1\}^N\). Each \(\omega=(\omega_1,\dots,\omega_N)\in\Omega\) is one full realization of the \(N\) events. For each state \(\omega\), let \(P_\omega\) denote the parlay that selects outcome \(\omega_j\) on event \(j\) for every \(j\). Its gross decimal multiplier is \(D_\omega=\prod_{j=1}^N d_{j,\omega_j}\). Let \(x_\omega\ge 0\) be the amount of bonus money staked on parlay \(P_\omega\), subject to the budget constraint \(\sum_{\omega\in\Omega} x_\omega = B\). Because bonus stakes aren't returned, if state \(\omega\) occurs, the cash conversion generated by the full strategy is

$$
C(\omega;x)=x_\omega(D_\omega-1).
$$

Exactly one state occurs, so exactly one of the \(2^N\) state-specific parlays wins. The bettor's objective is to maximize the guaranteed conversion across states:

$$
\max_{x_\omega\ge 0}\ \min_{\omega\in\Omega} x_\omega(D_\omega-1)
\qquad
\text{subject to}
\qquad
\sum_{\omega\in\Omega}x_\omega=B.
$$

## Optimal Stake Allocation

The optimal full state-space hedge (i.e., the maximum guaranteed cash conversion) in the general case is

$$
C^*
=
\frac{B}{\displaystyle\sum_{\omega\in\Omega} 1/(D_\omega-1)}.
$$

The unique optimal stake allocation is

$$
x_\omega^*
=
\frac{C^*}{D_\omega-1}
=
\frac{B\,[1/(D_\omega-1)]}{\displaystyle\sum_{\nu\in\Omega} 1/(D_\nu-1)}.
$$

The **proof** is as follows. Suppose some feasible allocation \(x\) attains guaranteed conversion \(C\), so that \(x_\omega(D_\omega-1)\ge C\) for every \(\omega\). Then \(x_\omega \ge C/(D_\omega-1)\) for every \(\omega\).
Summing over all states gives

$$
B=\sum_{\omega\in\Omega}x_\omega
\ge
\sum_{\omega\in\Omega} C/(D_\omega-1)
=
C\sum_{\omega\in\Omega} 1/(D_\omega-1).
$$

Hence every feasible guaranteed conversion must satisfy

$$
C \le \frac{B}{\sum_{\omega\in\Omega} 1/(D_\omega-1)}.
$$

Now define

$$
x_\omega^*=\frac{C^*}{D_\omega-1},
\qquad
C^*=\frac{B}{\sum_{\omega\in\Omega} 1/(D_\omega-1)}.
$$

Then \(\sum_{\omega\in\Omega}x_\omega^*=B\), and for every \(\omega\),

$$
x_\omega^*(D_\omega-1)=C^*.
$$

So this allocation is feasible and attains the upper bound. Therefore it's optimal. Uniqueness follows because any optimal allocation must satisfy \(x_\omega(D_\omega-1)=C^*\) for every \(\omega\), which determines each \(x_\omega\) uniquely. \(\square\)

The optimal stake on a state-specific parlay is inversely proportional to that parlay's net multiplier \(D_\omega-1\). Higher-paying parlays receive smaller stakes, while lower-paying parlays must receive larger stakes in order to equalize the guaranteed cash conversion across states.

## Symmetric Equal-Stake Case

A particularly simple case arises when each event is symmetric, meaning \(d_{j,0}=d_{j,1}=d_j\) for every \(j\). Then \(D_\omega=\prod_{j=1}^N d_j\) is the same for every \(\omega\in\Omega\). It follows immediately from the proposition that equal stakes are optimal:

$$
x_\omega^*=B/2^N
\qquad
\text{for every } \omega\in\Omega.
$$

The guaranteed conversion is then

$$
C^*
=
\frac{B}{2^N}\left(\prod_{j=1}^N d_j - 1\right).
$$

If, in addition, all legs have the same decimal odds \(d\), then \(\prod_{j=1}^N d_j=d^N\), and the conversion rate becomes

$$
\frac{C^*}{B}=\frac{d^N-1}{2^N}.
$$

This is the basic symmetric benchmark for the parlay hedge.

## No-Vig and Vig-Distorted Benchmarks

The no-vigorish benchmark is the case of fair pick'em legs, where each binary event is priced at \(d=2\). Then

$$
\frac{C^*}{B}=\frac{2^N-1}{2^N}=1-2^{-N}.
$$

So the guaranteed conversion rate is \(50\%\) when \(N=1\), \(75\%\) when \(N=2\), \(87.5\%\) when \(N=3\), \(93.75\%\) when \(N=4\), and \(96.875\%\) when \(N=5\). In particular, as \(N\to\infty\), the guaranteed conversion rate approaches \(100\%\) of the bonus face value.

The more realistic benchmark is a vig-distorted symmetric market with decimal odds \(d<2\) on both sides of each binary event. In that case the conversion rate remains

$$
\frac{C^*}{B}=\frac{d^N-1}{2^N},
$$

but its behavior changes materially. For example, when \(d=1.91\), the guaranteed conversion rate is about \(45.5\%\) for \(N=1\), \(66.2\%\) for \(N=2\), \(74.6\%\) for \(N=3\), \(76.9\%\) for \(N=4\), and \(76.3\%\) for \(N=5\). Thus, unlike the no-vig case, adding more legs isn't always better once vigorish is present.

This difference is economically important. In the no-vig benchmark, the full state-space hedge can make the bonus almost equivalent to cash if enough binary events are used. Under realistic sportsbook pricing, however, the bookmaker's margin compounds through the parlay structure and eventually offsets the gains from covering a larger outcome space.

## Optimal Number of Legs in the Symmetric Case

In the symmetric vig-distorted benchmark, define \(c_N(d)=(d^N-1)/2^N\) for \(d\in(1,2)\).
Treating \(N\) as a continuous variable, we solve the optimization problem:

$$
\frac{d}{dN}c_N(d)=2^{-N}\bigl(d^N\ln d-(d^N-1)\ln 2\bigr)=0
$$

$$
d^N\ln d=(d^N-1)\ln 2
$$

$$
d^N=\ln 2/(\ln 2-\ln d).
$$

Hence the continuous optimizer is

$$
N^*
=
\ln\!\bigl(\ln 2/(\ln 2-\ln d)\bigr)/\ln d.
$$

For \(d=1.91\), this gives \(N^*\approx 4.19\), so the best integer choice is \(N=4\). This recovers the numerical pattern above and shows that, under standard sportsbook-style pricing, the most efficient symmetric parlay hedge will generally use only a moderate number of legs.

The symmetric case isn't merely convenient. Under a fixed level of vigorish on each binary event, it is in fact **optimal**. For each event \(j\), define the two-way overround by \(s_j = 1/d_{j,0} + 1/d_{j,1}\) for \(s_j>1\). Holding the values \(s_1,\dots,s_N\) fixed, we ask which choice of odds maximizes the guaranteed conversion \(C^*\).

For fixed per-leg overrounds \(s_1,\dots,s_N\), the guaranteed conversion \(C^*\) is maximized when each event is symmetric: \(d_{j,0}=d_{j,1}=2/s_j\) for every \(j\).

The **proof** is as follows. Notice that maximizing \(C^*\) is equivalent to minimizing \(\sum_{\omega\in\Omega} 1/(D_\omega-1)\). Fix an event \(j\), and hold the odds on all other events fixed. For any fixed realization of the other \(N-1\) events, let \(z=\prod_{i\ne j} d_{i,\omega_i}\).
The two states that differ only on event \(j\) contribute \(1/(z\,d_{j,0}-1) + 1/(z\,d_{j,1}-1)\) to the sum. Now let \(x=1/d_{j,0}\) and \(y=1/d_{j,1}\), so \(x+y=s_j\). Then the pair contribution becomes \(x/(z-x) + y/(z-y)\). Define \(\phi_z(t)=t/(z-t)\). Since \(\phi_z''(t)=2z/(z-t)^3>0\), the function \(\phi_z\) is convex. Therefore, subject to the constraint \(x+y=s_j\), the sum \(\phi_z(x)+\phi_z(y)\) is minimized when \(x=y=s_j/2\). Equivalently, \(1/d_{j,0}=1/d_{j,1}=s_j/2,\) which means \(d_{j,0}=d_{j,1}=2/s_j.\) Because this argument applies to each event \(j\) separately, the full denominator \(\sum_{\omega\in\Omega} 1/(D_\omega-1)\) is minimized when every leg is symmetric. Hence \(C^*\) is maximized in that case. \(\square\)

This proposition formalizes a useful practical implication: if the bookmaker's margin is held fixed, shopping for more asymmetric plus-money/minus-money combinations doesn't improve the full state-space parlay hedge. The best version of the strategy uses events that are as close to pick'em as possible.

As a corollary, if every event has the same overround \(s\), then the optimal symmetric price is \(d=2/s\), and the guaranteed conversion rate becomes

$$
\frac{C^*}{B}
=
\frac{(2/s)^N-1}{2^N}.
$$

This nests both earlier benchmarks: when \(s=1\), it reduces to the no-vig formula \(1-2^{-N}\); when \(s>1\), it gives the corresponding vig-distorted optimum.

The full state-space parlay hedge differs from the one-event hedge in an important way. It doesn't rely on adding a real-money bet on the opposite side of a single market. Instead, it uses the bonus money itself to span the entire outcome space. In the symmetric case, this produces a very simple rule: split the bonus equally across all \(2^N\) state-specific parlays. In the general case, the correct rule is only slightly more complicated: allocate stakes inversely to the parlays' net multipliers.

The economic lesson is that bonus money can sometimes be converted far more efficiently by covering an entire set of possible states than by hedging a single event. Under realistic \(-110\)-style pricing, the best one-event hedge converts only about \(61\%\) of the bonus face value into risk-free cash, whereas the full state-space parlay hedge can do materially better when \(N\) is chosen well. Its practical drawback, of course, is that it requires the bonus to be divisible and spread across many tickets, which may not be feasible under all promotional terms.
